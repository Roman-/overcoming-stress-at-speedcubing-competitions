# How to Stop Your Hands from Shaking at Competitions

- Video: https://youtu.be/98y88lXtafA
- Mind Map: https://app.xmind.com/Ngc9KfTN

---

Your hands shake at competitions because your brain thinks something is different. And it is right. Everything is different. The noise, the people, the chair, the lighting, the fact that you only get five solves. Your brain goes into panic mode because none of this looks like your comfortable desk at home where you did a hundred solves yesterday.

The fix is simple: make your home practice feel like a competition. If nothing feels new when you sit down at the competition table, your brain has no reason to panic.

There are two sides to this. One is your environment, which is all the physical stuff around you. The other is your head, which is the feeling that these five solves are somehow special. Both need fixing. Below are a bunch of practical things you can do at home, starting with the easy ones and getting progressively weirder.

---

## Easy stuff you can start today

**Play competition noise in the background while you practice.** Just find a video of a busy competition on YouTube and let it play. The talking, the timer beeps, the announcements, the chair scraping. Your brain currently treats all that noise as a threat. If you hear it every day, it becomes boring. That is exactly what you want.

**Move your light source before each practice session.** One of the most annoying things at competitions is that the lighting is always different and your stickers suddenly look like different colors. Get a desk lamp you can move around. Put it on the left today, on the right tomorrow, behind you the day after. After a while your brain stops caring about how the shadows fall on your cube.

**Practice in different spots around your house.** Do some solves at the kitchen table. Do some standing up. Do some on the couch. Do some at a desk you never use. At competitions, the table and chair are always random. If you only ever practice at your one perfect desk, your brain links good performance to that specific spot. Break that link.

**Change your chair height or just use a different chair.** Competition chairs are almost never the same height as yours at home. Sometimes they are too low, sometimes too high. If you only practice in your perfect ergonomic setup, any change will feel wrong. Sit on a stool sometimes. Sit on a dining chair. Sit on something that is a little annoying.

**Use strict 15-second inspection every time.** A lot of people get sloppy with inspection at home. They glance at the cube for 3 seconds or stare for 30. At competition you get exactly 15 seconds and a judge is watching. Use a timer. Be strict. Make the real inspection feel normal.

---

## A bit more effort but still normal

**Stream your practice sessions.** Turn on a camera, go live on YouTube or Twitch, and just solve. Even if zero people are watching. The point is that the link is public and someone could show up at any time. This trains your brain to be okay with being watched. After a few weeks the camera becomes like furniture. You forget it is there. And that is exactly the goal: being watched should feel like nothing.

**Do the full competition routine for some of your solves.** After you scramble, cover your cube. Say "ready" out loud or nod like the judge is there. Lift the cover and start inspection. After the solve, grab a piece of paper and sign it like a scorecard. You do not need to do this for every solve. If you do 50 solves a day, maybe 10 of them get the full routine. Enough to make the ritual boring so it does not surprise you at a real competition.

**Limit yourself to exactly 5 counted solves.** This is a big one. At home you can always do one more. At competition you get five and that is it. So sometimes just do five solves, calculate your average, and stop. Walk away. No extra attempts. No "that scramble was unfair." Just five. This teaches your brain that five solves is not a rare event, it is just Tuesday.

**Add random waiting between solves.** At competition you do not control when your next solve starts. You wait. Sometimes a little, sometimes a lot. Set a random timer between 30 seconds and 3 minutes between your practice solves. Sit there and do nothing. No warmup solves. Just wait. This is surprisingly hard and surprisingly useful.

**Practice with a friend judging you.** If you have a friend or family member willing to spend five minutes, ask them to sit across from you and act as a judge. They say "ready," you nod, they start the timer. They do not even need to know how to cube. They just need to sit there and watch you. Having a human watching you from 30 centimeters away is very different from being alone.

## Getting a bit weird now

**Put a video of people walking around on a screen next to you.** Find a long video of a crowded place, a mall, an airport, a busy street. Play it on a tablet or monitor right at the edge of your vision while you solve. Your peripheral vision will pick up the movement and your brain will flag it as a distraction. After doing this enough times, the movement of people at a competition will not bother you.

**Do solves in public places.** Go to a cafe, a library, a park bench. Pull out your cube and do timed solves. People will look at you. Some might talk to you. It will feel awkward at first. Good. That awkwardness is exactly the muscle you need to build. Once you can solve comfortably while a stranger watches you over their coffee, competition spectators are nothing.

**Do jumping jacks before a solve.** Your heart rate goes up at competition because of adrenaline. You can simulate that by doing 20 seconds of jumping jacks or pushups right before you start a solve. Your hands will be slightly shaky, your heart will be beating faster, and now you practice solving in exactly that state. This is weirdly effective.

**Run many fake rounds at home.** Pick a time, like 3pm on Saturday, sit down, and pretend it is the first round. Do exactly five solves with full routine, random waiting, scorecard signing, everything. Calculate your average. Then get up and walk away for 20 minutes. Come back and do another round. This is basically a home competition. The more fake rounds you do, the less special the real ones feel.

**After doing 50 solves, randomly pick 5 and pretend those were your competition round.** This is a mental trick. You do your normal session, then afterwards you roll a die or use a random number generator to pick five of those solves. Those are now your "official" results. This attacks the feeling that competition solves need to be approached differently. They were just five normal solves that happened to be selected.

---

## Okay this is getting weird but it actually works

**Practice in uncomfortable temperatures.** Open the window when it is cold outside and do a few solves with chilly hands. Or put on an extra sweater and practice when you are slightly too warm. Competition venues are never the perfect temperature. If you only practice in your perfectly heated room, any temperature change will throw you off. A few sets per day in slightly uncomfortable conditions builds adaptability.

**Set up an AI judge voice or a text-to-speech app that calls your name at random times.** Record or generate a voice that says things like "competitor 47, you are up" or "ready?" and play it at random intervals during your session. This simulates the startle of being called to your station. The first few times you will flinch. After a while you will not even notice.

**Practice with one eye closed or with sunglasses on.** This is not about making yourself worse. It is about training your brain to recognize pieces under bad conditions. If you can still solve well when visibility is deliberately terrible, slightly weird competition lighting will not even register.

**Organize a full fake competition in your living room.** Print a name badge. Make a fake schedule. Set up a waiting area, which is just a different chair. Have someone call you to the solving station. Do five solves with full ritual. Calculate your average. Announce your result out loud to nobody. This sounds absolutely ridiculous but the more seriously you take it, the more your brain files it as a real competition experience. And the more real competition experiences you have, the less special the actual competition feels.

---

## The mental side

All the stuff above handles the environment. But there is another half: your brain telling you that these five solves are special.

**They are not.**

If you did 100 solves yesterday, those five competition solves are just five more. Statistically they are meaningless. One solve barely moves your lifetime average. But your brain treats them like a final exam. And when you treat a solve as important, you start consciously controlling finger tricks that should be automatic. That is when you choke.

**The trick is to not care.** I know that sounds stupid. You trained for months, you traveled to the competition, of course you care. But when you sit down at the table, your job is to pretend you do not. Think about the afterparty. Think about the pizza you are going to eat. Think about the board games you will play with friends later. Think about literally anything other than the solve.

**Your goal is to make those five competition solves psychologically identical to any five random solves from your practice session.** No solve gets special treatment. No solve gets emotional weight. Every scramble is just a scramble.

The paradox is that caring less gives you a better chance of showing your actual average. Because your actual average was built in a state where you did not care. You were just turning the cube. Do that again.

---

## If you only do three things

1. Stream your solves so being watched feels normal.
2. Do the full competition routine for at least a few solves every day.
3. At competition, think about the afterparty, not the solve.

Pick one thing from this page. Try it today. Add more over time.

Make competition feel boring. That is when you perform your best.
